Understanding Overconsumption

Overconsumption is the excessive consumption or use of goods and services (energy, land, water or materials) that cause harm or detrimental effects to humans and/or the environment, namely by exceeding the carrying capacity and life supporting systems of the planet and its ecosystems.

It's a growing global concern that affects our environment, society, and future generations.

The Problem at a Glance

Resource Depletion

Many natural resources are being consumed faster than they can be replenished, including forests, fisheries, and freshwater sources.

Waste Generation

Growing consumption leads to more waste, much of which is not properly recycled or disposed of, leading to pollution.

Climate Impact

Increased production and consumption of goods contributes significantly to greenhouse gas emissions and climate change.

Ecological Footprint vs. Biocapacity

The ecological footprint measures human demand on nature, comparing the resources people consume with the earth's ability to regenerate them.

When ecological footprint exceeds biocapacity, it means we're using resources faster than they can be renewed, leading to environmental degradation.

Key Facts About Overconsumption

  • If everyone lived like the average American, we would need 5 Earths to sustain our resource use.
  • About 1/3 of all food produced globally is wasted.
  • The richest 10% of people are responsible for approximately 50% of global emissions.
  • Since 1970, the global population has doubled, but resource extraction has tripled.
  • Fast fashion leads to 92 million tons of textile waste annually.

Why This Matters

Overconsumption is not just an environmental issue—it affects social equity, economic stability, and the well-being of future generations. Understanding our consumption patterns is the first step toward making positive changes.

"The Earth has enough resources for everyone's needs, but not for everyone's greed." — Mahatma Gandhi
